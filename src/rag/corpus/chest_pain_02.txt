# SYNTHETIC CASE — generated for RAG pipeline testing

A 67-year-old man describes a tight, pressure-like ache across the center of
his chest that comes on whenever he climbs the two flights of stairs to his
apartment or walks uphill in cold weather. The discomfort lasts a few minutes,
never more than five, and reliably goes away when he stops and rests. It has
been happening for about eight months and follows a predictable pattern. He
denies pain at rest and denies sweating or nausea during episodes.

He has known coronary artery disease, diagnosed after an angiogram three years
ago, along with hypertension and hyperlipidemia. He takes aspirin, a statin,
metoprolol, and keeps a nitroglycerin spray that he uses "maybe once a week."
He is a retired plumber, walks his dog daily, and quit smoking in his forties.

His manner is calm, almost resigned. He has lived with this for so long that he
has learned to pace himself and plan his errands around it. He came in today
only because his wife insisted after he had two episodes in one morning. He is
mildly embarrassed about "making a fuss."

The underlying cause is stable exertional angina from fixed coronary stenosis.
The hallmark is its predictability: brought on by a reproducible level of
exertion and promptly relieved by rest, distinguishing it from an acute
coronary event.
