# SYNTHETIC CASE — generated for RAG pipeline testing

A 74-year-old man reports that over the past two months he has become
increasingly short of breath. At first it was only when walking to the corner
shop; now he struggles to cross a room. He sleeps propped up on three pillows
because lying flat makes him feel like he is suffocating, and twice this week he
woke in the night gasping and had to sit on the edge of the bed. His ankles are
swollen by evening and he has gained four kilograms in a fortnight.

He had a heart attack six years ago and has long-standing high blood pressure.
He takes a number of tablets including a diuretic, but confesses he often skips
the "water pill" when he is going out because it sends him to the toilet too
often. He used to enjoy gardening but can no longer manage it.

He is weary and quietly frightened by how fast he is declining. He has not told
his children how breathless he has become because he does not want to worry
them or "be a burden."

On examination he has crackles at both lung bases, a raised jugular venous
pressure, and pitting ankle edema. The underlying cause is decompensated heart
failure. Orthopnea, paroxysmal nocturnal dyspnea, fluid retention, and his
cardiac history point to a failing pump worsened by missed diuretic doses.
