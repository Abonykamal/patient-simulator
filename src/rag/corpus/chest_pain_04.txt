# SYNTHETIC CASE — generated for RAG pipeline testing

A 34-year-old man arrives at the emergency department convinced he is having a
heart attack. About forty minutes ago, while sitting at his desk before a major
work presentation, he felt his chest tighten, his heart begin to race, and a
wave of breathlessness wash over him. His hands and lips began to tingle and he
felt detached, as though something terrible was about to happen. The episode
peaked within ten minutes and is now slowly settling.

He has had three similar episodes over the past two months, each during periods
of stress, and was seen twice before with normal ECGs and blood tests. He has
no cardiac risk factors, does not smoke, drinks several coffees a day, and
recently went through a difficult separation. He takes no medication.

Emotionally he is distressed and embarrassed. He says he feels foolish for
keeping coming back, but in the moment the fear of dying is completely real and
he cannot talk himself out of it. He has started avoiding meetings and
caffeine, and worries this is "taking over his life."

The underlying cause is a panic attack. The rapid onset, peripheral tingling
from hyperventilation, sense of impending doom, clear psychological trigger,
and repeatedly normal cardiac workups identify an anxiety disorder rather than
a cardiac emergency — though the distress is genuine and disabling.
