# SYNTHETIC CASE — generated for RAG pipeline testing

A 63-year-old woman presents to the emergency department with crushing
substernal chest pain that began ninety minutes ago while she was carrying
groceries. The pain radiates to her jaw and left arm and is accompanied by
profuse sweating, nausea, and a feeling of breathlessness. She rates it 9 out
of 10 and says it has not eased with rest. Her skin is cool and clammy.

She has a history of hypertension and high cholesterol, both treated, and was
told two years ago she was "borderline diabetic." She smoked half a pack a day
for thirty years and quit only last year. Her father died of a heart attack at
sixty. She takes amlodipine and a statin but admits she sometimes forgets the
statin on weekends.

Emotionally she is frightened but keeps insisting it is "probably just bad
indigestion from lunch," and she initially resisted her daughter's decision to
call an ambulance. She is worried about who will look after her husband, who
has dementia, if she is admitted.

The underlying cause is an acute ST-elevation myocardial infarction. The
combination of classic radiating pain, diaphoresis, autonomic symptoms, and a
dense cardiac risk-factor profile points to an evolving infarct requiring
emergent reperfusion.
