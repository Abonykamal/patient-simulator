# SYNTHETIC CASE — generated for RAG pipeline testing

A 48-year-old woman presents with two days of swelling and pain in her left
calf. The leg feels tight, warm, and tender to touch, and the discomfort
worsens when she walks or flexes her foot. The calf looks visibly larger and
slightly redder than the right. There is no injury she can recall and no
swelling on the other side.

She returned five days ago from a long-haul flight home from a holiday, around
eleven hours sitting with little chance to move. She takes the combined oral
contraceptive pill and smokes around ten cigarettes a day. She is otherwise
well and works as a hairdresser, on her feet most of the day.

She is worried and on edge. A colleague's husband recently died suddenly from a
blood clot that "started in the leg," and she keeps asking whether the same
thing could happen to her. She is frightened but also feels slightly foolish for
"making a fuss over a swollen leg."

On examination the left calf is swollen with a measurable difference in
circumference, warm, and tender along the deep veins. The underlying cause is a
deep vein thrombosis. The unilateral warm, tender, swollen calf in the setting
of recent immobility, oestrogen-containing contraception, and smoking points to
venous thromboembolism, with the important risk of progression to pulmonary
embolism.
