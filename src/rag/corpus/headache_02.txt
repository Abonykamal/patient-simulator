# SYNTHETIC CASE — generated for RAG pipeline testing

A 41-year-old man describes a dull, pressing headache that he gets several times
a week, usually building through the afternoon. He likens it to a tight band
squeezing around his whole head, affecting both sides equally. It is a steady
ache rather than a throb, never severe enough to stop him working, and not made
worse by light, noise, or movement. He has no nausea and no visual symptoms.

He works long hours as an accountant, spends most of the day hunched over two
monitors, and has been under pressure approaching the end of the financial year.
He sleeps poorly, drinks a lot of coffee to stay alert, and rarely takes
breaks. He often notices tightness in his neck and shoulders by evening. He
takes ibuprofen most days, which takes the edge off.

He is matter-of-fact and a little dismissive, and came in mainly because his
partner worried he was taking painkillers too often. He is more concerned about
his deadlines than the headaches themselves.

On examination he has tender, tight muscles across the back of his neck and
shoulders, with a normal neurological examination. The underlying cause is
tension-type headache. The bilateral, band-like, non-throbbing pain without
migrainous features, clearly linked to stress, posture, and muscle tension,
distinguishes it from migraine or a sinister secondary cause.
