# SYNTHETIC CASE — generated for RAG pipeline testing

A 52-year-old woman describes a burning pain high in her abdomen, just below the
breastbone, that has troubled her on and off for several months. It is worst
after large meals and when she lies down at night, and she often wakes with a
sour, acid taste at the back of her throat. Sitting up and chewing antacid
tablets helps within minutes. Spicy food, coffee, and red wine reliably bring
it on.

She works long hours managing a busy restaurant, eats late, and has gained
weight over the past two years. She takes no regular medication beyond the
over-the-counter antacids she now keeps in every handbag. She drinks two or
three coffees a day and a glass of wine most evenings.

She is dismissive of the whole thing, convinced it is "just stress and bad
eating," and only came because a friend frightened her by mentioning that
heartburn can sometimes be the heart. She is impatient and keen to get back to
work.

On examination her abdomen is soft with mild epigastric tenderness and no
alarming features. The underlying cause is gastro-oesophageal reflux disease.
The burning retrosternal pain, acid regurgitation, nocturnal and postural
aggravation, and clear dietary triggers, all relieved by antacids, point to
reflux rather than a cardiac or surgical cause.
