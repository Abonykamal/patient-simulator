# SYNTHETIC CASE — generated for RAG pipeline testing

A 70-year-old man presents with swelling of both legs that has crept up over the
past few weeks. The swelling is worst around his ankles by the end of the day
and eases a little overnight. Pressing on his shins leaves a dent that slowly
fills back in. He has also noticed he gets breathless climbing stairs and needs
an extra pillow at night to sleep comfortably. There is no pain, redness, or
warmth in either leg.

He has long-standing high blood pressure and coronary artery disease, with a
stent placed four years ago. He takes several heart tablets and a water tablet,
though he admits he is not always good at remembering them. He has gained weight
recently despite eating less.

He is weary and inclined to brush it off as "just getting old," and his wife
finally persuaded him to come. He is more bothered by the breathlessness than
the legs, and quietly worried about becoming dependent.

On examination he has symmetrical pitting edema to mid-shin, a raised jugular
venous pressure, and fine crackles at both lung bases. The underlying cause is
fluid overload from heart failure. The bilateral, painless, dependent pitting
edema combined with orthopnea, exertional breathlessness, and his cardiac
history points to a systemic cause rather than a local venous one.
