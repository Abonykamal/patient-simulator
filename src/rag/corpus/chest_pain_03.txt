# SYNTHETIC CASE — generated for RAG pipeline testing

A 29-year-old woman comes to an urgent care clinic worried about a sharp pain
on the left side of her chest that has been present for three days. The pain is
well localized to a spot near her breastbone, feels stabbing rather than
heavy, and gets noticeably worse when she takes a deep breath, twists her
torso, or presses on the area. Lying still helps. She has no sweating,
no nausea, and no shortness of breath at rest.

She is otherwise healthy, takes no regular medication, does not smoke, and has
no family history of early heart disease. She recently started an intensive
weightlifting program at a new gym and had a particularly heavy chest workout
four days ago. She also moved house last weekend and carried boxes for hours.

She is visibly anxious. She searched her symptoms online, read about heart
attacks in young people, and has barely slept worrying she is dying. She keeps
apologizing for "probably overreacting" but says the fear has been
overwhelming.

On examination the pain is reproduced exactly by pressing on the
costochondral junctions. The underlying cause is musculoskeletal chest
pain — costochondritis following unaccustomed exertion. The reproducibility on
palpation and the positional, pleuritic quality point away from a cardiac
source despite the patient's intense fear.
