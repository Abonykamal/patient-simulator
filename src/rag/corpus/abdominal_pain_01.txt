# SYNTHETIC CASE — generated for RAG pipeline testing

A 19-year-old man presents with abdominal pain that began about eighteen hours
ago. It started as a vague ache around his navel, but over the last several
hours it has shifted to the right lower side and become sharp and constant.
Walking, coughing, and the car journey to hospital all made it worse. He has no
appetite, felt nauseated, and vomited once. He thinks he may have a slight
fever.

He is a first-year engineering student, normally fit and well, on no
medication, and has never been in hospital before. He plays football twice a
week but could not face training today.

He is frightened and clearly out of his depth. This is the first time he has
dealt with a medical problem away from his parents, and he keeps asking whether
he is going to need surgery and whether someone has called his mother. He is
trying to be brave but is visibly close to tears.

On examination he lies still, reluctant to move, with tenderness and guarding in
the right iliac fossa and pain on releasing pressure there. His temperature is
mildly raised. The underlying cause is acute appendicitis. The classic
migration of pain from the umbilicus to the right lower quadrant, anorexia,
low-grade fever, and localized peritoneal signs indicate an inflamed appendix
requiring urgent surgical assessment.
